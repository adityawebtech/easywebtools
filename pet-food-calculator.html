Pet Nutrition Guidelines by Species

Dogs

Dogs are America’s #1 most popular pet, with over 65 million households owning at least one in 2024. Proper feeding is based on well-established metabolic science. Our calculation uses:
RER = 70 × (weight in kg)0.75, then MER = RER × a factor for activity and age.
For example, adult neutered dogs need about 1.6 × RER, while puppies or very active breeds can require much more. Consistently monitoring your dog’s weight and body condition is the best way to confirm proper meal size.

Cats

With 38 million US households sharing their lives with cats, a nutritious, portion-controlled diet is absolutely vital. Cats require higher protein and specific fats compared to most pets. Our calculator lets you select kitten, adult, or senior—because kittens may need up to 2.5 × RER, and older cats often require fewer calories.
Did you know? Over 59% of American cats are overweight. Feeding to maintain an ideal body condition helps avoid diabetes and joint problems later in life.

Rabbits

Rabbits make up one of the most popular “exotic” pets in the US. Unlike dogs or cats, rabbits need a fiber-rich, mostly grass hay diet to keep their digestive system healthy. Fresh hay (like timothy or orchard grass) should be provided at all times—it should make up at least 70% of their intake. Only about 1/8 to 1/4 cup of high-quality pellets per 5 pounds of body weight are appropriate for healthy adults. Leafy greens and very limited treats (no more than 5% of the daily diet) support optimal health.
Sudden changes in diet or excessive pellets/treats are major risk factors for obesity and GI stasis. Clean, fresh water is a must.

Guinea Pigs

Beloved for their gentle nature, guinea pigs require constant access to grass hay and fresh vitamin C-rich veggies (since, like humans, they cannot make vitamin C). Feed about 1/8 cup of fortified pellets per 2 lbs, plus a large handful of clean hay daily. Offer bell peppers and small amounts of leafy greens for vitamins. Monitor their weight monthly on a kitchen scale—guinea pigs are prone to “slow” weight gain, which is hard to spot visually. Always avoid lettuce with high water content (like iceberg) and sugary snacks.

Hamster

Hamsters are small but energetic. Nutrition is based on specially formulated hamster mixes—about 1 to 2 tablespoons daily for most adults, supplemented with small veggies and an occasional protein treat (like a cooked egg piece). Sudden weight loss, greasy coat, or inactivity may signal improper feeding. Fresh water, offered in a bottle and checked daily, is just as vital as food variety.

Bird

Birds like parakeets (budgies), cockatiels, and finches are among America’s favorite companions. The healthiest diets now go far beyond “seeds,” focusing on quality pellets (60–70% of intake), supplemented by fresh greens, a little fruit, and clean water daily. Typical portion: 1.5–2 tablespoons of pellets for most small parrots, with variety adjusted by species. Obesity and vitamin A/calcium deficiencies are common with all-seed diets, so variety is key. Be mindful—some foods (avocado, chocolate, caffeine) are toxic to birds.

Fish

Fish are “the silent majority” in US pet ownership (over 11 million households). The golden rule: Feed only what your fish can eat in 2–3 minutes, once or twice daily. Overfeeding is far more dangerous than underfeeding—leftover food fouls water, causing spikes in ammonia that harm all tank life. Portion guidance varies by species: bettas require about 2–3 pellets twice daily, while goldfish may need a pinch of flakes. Fast one day per week to prevent obesity and digestive problems.

Reptiles

Reptiles (like bearded dragons, turtles, snakes) have widely varying needs. For most, meal sizes and frequency are based on age, species, and whether they eat plants, meat, or both. For example, adult bearded dragons eat mainly leafy greens plus insects a few times weekly, while corn snakes are fed a single, body-width-appropriate rodent every 10–14 days as adults. Overfeeding is a leading cause of illness in pet reptiles; always mimic natural feeding rhythms for health and longevity. UVB lighting and water access are essential for nutrient use.

Science Behind the Tool

This calculator is based on:

  • Resting Energy Requirement (RER): RER = 70 × (body weight in kg)0.75
  • Maintenance Energy Requirement (MER): Multiplies RER by a species- and life-stage-dependent factor (1.2–3.0+)
  • Species-specific conversion tables for birds, rabbits, guinea pigs, fish, etc.
  • Portion recommendations rely on typical US-brand pet food densities (foods per cup, per can, or per typical gram/pellet serving)

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I’m feeding my pet enough?

Track your pet’s weight monthly and ensure ribs are easily felt but not visible. Our calculator gives a trusted starting range, but always consult your veterinarian for fine-tuning. Regular weigh-ins are proven to prevent silent weight gain or loss.

What’s the healthiest diet for my pet?

Choose foods formulated to meet AAFCO standards for your pet’s species and age. Mix up fresh, commercially formulated, or prescription diets as advised by your vet.

Can I use this calculator for wet/raw diets?

You can—it provides results in grams as well as cups. For home-cooked or raw, use the kcal total, weighing accordingly. Always check with a veterinary nutritionist first when changing diet types.

How often should I feed my pet?

Most dogs and cats do best with two meals per day. Puppies, kittens, and small mammals may require three or four, while some reptiles or fish may only need feeding every 1–3 days. The tool helps guide by species.

Is this tool safe for pets with health problems?

Only as a general reference. Pets with diabetes, kidney disease, allergies, or on special diets must follow your veterinarian's personalized advice above all else.

Should food be changed as my pet ages?

Yes! Caloric needs drop with most senior pets. Our calculator flags when a change of group could impact portion sizes.

How accurate is the data?

Our calculators implement published US nutrition protocols, and our team reviews/updates all numbers yearly in July with the latest data from AAFCO, AVMA, and ASPCA.
